The United States Senate

The Upper Chamber of the United States Congress

The United States Senate is the upper chamber of the United States Congress, with the House of Representatives being the lower chamber. Together they compose the national bicameral legislature of the United States. The Senate was designed by the Founders to be a more deliberative body, providing stability and representing the interests of the states.

About the Senate

Composition:
The Senate is composed of 100 senators, with two senators representing each of the 50 states. Unlike the House, Senate representation is equal regardless of population, giving each state an equal voice in this chamber.

Terms of Service:
Senators serve six-year terms, with elections staggered so that approximately one-third of the Senate is up for election every two years. Senators must be at least 30 years old, a U.S. citizen for at least nine years, and a resident of the state they represent.

Leadership:
The Vice President of the United States serves as the President of the Senate but only votes in case of a tie. The President pro tempore, traditionally the longest-serving member of the majority party, presides in the Vice President's absence. Other leadership includes the Majority Leader, Minority Leader, and party whips.

Exclusive Powers:
The Senate has several exclusive powers, including:
- Confirming presidential appointments (Cabinet members, federal judges, ambassadors)
- Ratifying treaties (requiring two-thirds majority)
- Conducting impeachment trials for officials impeached by the House
- Electing the Vice President in case of an Electoral College tie

The Filibuster:
The Senate is known for its tradition of unlimited debate, which can be used to delay or block legislation through a procedure known as the filibuster. Ending a filibuster requires a cloture vote of 60 senators.

Committees:
The Senate operates through a system of committees that consider legislation, conduct oversight, and hold confirmation hearings. Major committees include Appropriations, Finance, Foreign Relations, Judiciary, and Armed Services.
